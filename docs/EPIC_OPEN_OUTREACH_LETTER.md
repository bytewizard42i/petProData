Dear Open@Epic Team,

My name is John Santi, and I am reaching out to introduce a concept called **Safe Health Data**—a privacy-preserving healthcare data framework designed to give patients stronger control over their medical information while still enabling meaningful care coordination, medical safety improvements, and compliant scientific research.

Safe Health Data is built around a simple idea: patients should not have to choose between privacy and usefulness. Too often, healthcare systems force a tradeoff between protecting sensitive personal information and making that information usable for treatment, safety, research, and continuity of care. Safe Health Data is intended to help bridge that gap.

At its core, the model would leverage the privacy-preserving capabilities of the **Midnight Network** to support **selective disclosure**, **tamper-evident verification**, and **patient-consented data sharing**. In practical terms, this means a patient's sensitive health information could remain protected by default, while specific facts, permissions, attestations, or proofs could still be shared with authorized parties when needed.

This architecture could support a number of meaningful healthcare benefits. It can help reduce unnecessary exposure of personal medical data while still allowing essential access for treatment and coordination. It can enable aggregated and privacy-preserving scientific research without requiring every participant's raw private information to be broadly exposed. It can also support optional patient participation models in which individuals may choose to opt in to additional levels of data sharing for research, trials, or analytics in exchange for compensation or other approved benefits.

Safe Health Data is also designed to strengthen trust and accountability through **immutable on-chain verification** of key permissions, consent states, and verification artifacts. This does not require raw medical records to be stored publicly on-chain. Rather, it allows critical proofs, timestamps, and authorization events to be recorded in a way that enhances auditability, integrity, and confidence across workflows.

Another important feature is patient safety. A privacy-preserving system can still help identify potential **adverse drug interactions**, contraindications, and relevant treatment risks without requiring full exposure of a patient's entire medical history to every participant in the care process. In the same way, it can help surface **medical alternatives** or additional care options that patients and clinicians may wish to evaluate together.

Safe Health Data also introduces a portable identity and access framework that allows a patient's verified medical permissions and essential records to move securely with them across environments. Through a combination of biometrics, QR-enabled access, and soulbound KYC-linked identity credentials, a patient's information can remain securely tied to the patient rather than fragmented across institutions. In appropriate cases, essential permissions and verified records could even precede the patient en route to a hospital or emergency facility, helping accelerate care while preserving privacy. This approach may be especially meaningful for schools, camps, assisted living facilities, and other institutions that often bear the burden of holding highly sensitive healthcare information. Rather than directly custodying large volumes of private medical data, these organizations could rely on patient-authorized, privacy-preserving verification pathways that reduce custodial burden and help limit unnecessary liability exposure while still supporting safety and continuity of care.

More broadly, Safe Health Data is not intended to replace trusted clinical systems. It is intended to complement them by adding a privacy-preserving, patient-centered layer for consent, portability, verification, safety checks, selective disclosure, and research participation.
